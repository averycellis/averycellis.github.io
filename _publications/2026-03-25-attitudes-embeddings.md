Open-ended survey responses can contain richer information about political attitudes than standard closed-form questions, but translating that richness into tractable measurement has remained elusive. I investigate whether sentence-level embeddings of open-ended responses can address two related problems in the measurement of political opinion.

The first problem is measuring opinion similarity and stability, both within individuals across issues and between individuals on the same issue. I develop a validation framework for establishing that cosine similarity in embedding space captures positional agreement rather than mere linguistic overlap. The second is evaluating whether the high-dimensional latent structure of these embeddings maps onto the closed-form survey scales that undergird attitude research. I treat residual variation between the embedding-projected and closed-form responses as evidence of the coarsening that fixed-response items impose on underlying attitude structure.

Together, these contributions offer a measurement framework with direct implications for the study of ideological constraint and opinion similarity. If embeddings recover attitude structure that closed-form items suppress, the implications extend beyond measurement: surveys designed around fixed-response items may systematically underestimate the dimensionality and heterogeneity of mass opinion.
