Open-ended survey responses contain richer information about political attitudes than the closed-form items that dominate the field, but translating that richness into tractable measurement has remained elusive. This paper investigates whether sentence-level embeddings of open-ended responses can address two related problems in the measurement of political opinion.

The first is measuring opinion similarity — both within individuals across issues and between individuals on the same issue. I develop a validation framework for establishing that cosine similarity in embedding space captures positional agreement rather than mere linguistic overlap, adapting crowdsourced Turing-test methods from the word embeddings literature to the specific task of opinion proximity. The second is evaluating whether the high-dimensional latent structure of these embeddings maps onto the closed-form survey scales that conventional attitude research relies on. I treat residual variation between the embedding-projected and closed-form responses as evidence of the coarsening that fixed-response items impose on underlying attitude structure.

Together, these contributions offer a novel measurement framework with direct implications for the study of ideological constraint and opinion similarity. If embeddings recover attitude structure that closed-form items suppress, the implications extend beyond measurement: surveys designed around fixed-response items may systematically underestimate the dimensionality and heterogeneity of mass opinion.
